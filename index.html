Contrast

Contrast is the difference between text and its background. Higher contrast makes text easier to read, especially for people with low vision.

High Contrast

This text has high contrast. The black text stands out clearly against the white background, making it easy to read.

Medium Contrast

This text has medium contrast. The gray text is less distinct from the background, requiring more effort to read.

Low Contrast

This text has low contrast. The light gray text blends with the background, making it difficult to read for many people.

For people with low vision, high contrast materials are essential. When designing accessible content, aim for a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text.

Ccolor

Ccolor is a custom color-vision demo inspired by color-dot tests like pseudo-isochromatic plates. This is not an official Ishihara® test and is not for diagnosis.

Astigmatism

This demo shows radial lines that can help identify astigmatism. This is not a substitute for professional eye examination.

Look at the center of the pattern. Do some lines appear darker, blurrier, or more distinct than others? If you notice differences, it may indicate astigmatism. Please see an eye care professional for proper testing and diagnosis.

Depth Perception

Depth perception allows us to judge distances and see the world in three dimensions.

Professional depth perception tests use specialized printed materials, polarized glasses, or stereoscopic devices to accurately measure depth perception. These tests require controlled lighting and viewing conditions.

Depth perception relies on several visual cues including:

  • Binocular vision: Using both eyes together to create depth
  • Stereopsis: The brain's ability to combine images from both eyes
  • Monocular cues: Shadows, overlapping objects, and perspective
